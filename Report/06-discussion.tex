\chapter{Discussion}
\label{ch:discussion}

This chapter interprets the results of Chapters~\ref{ch:verification} and~\ref{ch:results}, addressing the accuracy of the numerical implementation, the validity of the guiding-centre approximation, and the physical content of the planetary magnetosphere simulations.

\section{Solver Accuracy and Tolerances}
\label{sec:disc_validity}

The verification tests in Chapter~\ref{ch:verification} confirm that the solver correctly implements the Lorentz force across all field configurations tested. Energy conservation to better than $10^{-6}$ relative error in every static-field case, combined with agreement between numerical and analytic drift speeds to within 3\,\%, validates the chosen tolerances ($\texttt{rtol} = 10^{-9}$, $\texttt{atol} = 10^{-12}$).

The SI simulation in Section~\ref{sec:si_units} provides a more
demanding test. Integrating approximately 115{,}000 gyrations produces a relative energy drift of 1.5\,\% and a $\mu$ variation of 2\,\%, both growing secularly rather than oscillating. This is the expected signature of accumulated phase error in a non-symplectic integrator, not a failure of the physics. The mirror-point accuracy remains within 0.02\,\% of the analytic prediction throughout, confirming that the adiabatic invariant is well conserved even when the numerical phase drifts. For applications requiring long-term phase accuracy over many thousands of gyrations, a symplectic integrator such as the Boris scheme would be more appropriate.

\section{Validity of the Guiding-Centre Approximation}
\label{sec:disc_gc}

The guiding-centre approximation is valid when $\rho / L_{\nabla B} \ll 1$ and $\Omega^{-1} \ll T_b$. The results confirm this in two ways. First, conservation of $\mu$ below 1\,\% relative variation (Section~\ref{sec:mu}) shows that the adiabatic conditions are comfortably satisfied for the chosen parameters. Second, the direct comparison in Section~\ref{sec:gc_vs_full} shows that the GC integrator
tracks the gyro-averaged full orbit across four bounce cycles with no visible phase error in the bounce envelope, at a fraction of the computational cost.

The slow growth of the positional offset between the extracted GC and the GC equations solution is attributable to secular phase drift in the RK45 integrator rather than breakdown of the approximation itself: $\mu$ shows no secular trend, and the local agreement in $z(t)$ remains excellent throughout.

The approximation would be expected to break down at higher particle comparable to the field gradient scale. In such regimes, $\mu$ conservation would degrade and the full orbit integration would be necessary even to describe the slow drift motion.

\section{Tilted Dipole and Coordinate Effects}
\label{sec:disc_tilted}

The tilted dipole simulations (Section~\ref{sec:tilted}) demonstrate that the asymmetric bounce seen in geographic $z(t)$ at $47^\circ$ and $59^\circ$ tilt is a coordinate projection effect, not a physical asymmetry. In magnetic coordinates the bounce remains symmetric and the mirror latitudes are unchanged from the aligned case.

This distinction is relevant for interpreting spacecraft observations at planets with large magnetic tilt. At Neptune and Uranus, particle flux measurements in geographic coordinates would show an apparently asymmetric pitch-angle distribution even though the underlying dynamics are symmetric about the magnetic equator.

\section{Corotation and Drift Decomposition}
\label{sec:disc_corotation}

The corotation test (Section~\ref{sec:corotation}) recovers the input rotation rate $\Omega = 0.02$ to within 96.6\,\% by subtracting the magnetic drift contribution measured in a separate no-electric-field run. The 3.4\,\% shortfall is physical: the corotation velocity $\boldsymbol{\Omega} \times \mathbf{r}$ has a small component along $\mathbf{B}$ at non-equatorial latitudes, so the effective $\mathbf{E} \times \mathbf{B}$ drift is slightly less than $|\boldsymbol{\Omega} \times \mathbf{r}|$. A particle confined to the equatorial plane would co-rotate at exactly $\Omega$, but bounce motion
samples non-equatorial field geometries where this projection reduces the time-averaged drift.

\section{The Rotating Tilted Dipole}
\label{sec:disc_rotating}

The rotating tilted dipole (Section~\ref{sec:rotating}) combines a time-dependent magnetic field, corotation electric field, and three-dimensional particle dynamics into a single integration. The solver handles this without modification, simply evaluating $\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{r}, t)$ and $\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}, t)$ at each timestep.

The modulation of the bounce amplitude in Figure~\ref{fig:rot_z} is a direct consequence of the rotating field geometry and is absent in the static tilted case, confirming that it arises from the time dependence
of the field rather than the tilt itself. The measured azimuthal drift rate $\Omega_\mathrm{meas} \approx 0.026$ exceeds the rotation rate $\Omega = 0.02$, consistent with the additional gradient and curvature drift contribution measured in the aligned corotation test. This is the expected behaviour for trapped particles in a rotating magnetosphere.
